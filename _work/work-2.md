The Emergency Report assignment is a 5-12 page comprehensive Emergency Preparedness and Response plan for a selected library, which outlines "strategies for mitigating risks, responding to emergencies, and ensuring recovery and continuity of operations following a disaster." I chose to create a plan for the Webster County Genealogical Society (WCGS) in Fort Dodge, IA.


## [Webster County Genealogical Society Emergengy Plan (PDF)](/files/emergencyplan.pdf)

Creating the Emergency Plan for WCGS was not as difficult as I was worried it might be, but it did require a high level of thoughfulness and thoroughness, relying on professional resources to inform the specific actions and procedures contained in the plan. The data collection I did as part of an assessment project was very handy to already have, and because I wanted to be able to share this document with WCGS, I put in extra effort to make the content as clear as I could and to format it nicely.

This aligns with competence 2, Information Resources, especially 2A (Consider the issues related to the lifecycle of recorded knowledge and information, from creation through various stages of use, in relationship to material format and genre), and 2B (Apply the concepts, issues, and methods of collection management, which entails the lifecycle of materials from evaluation to long-term preservation and other curative practices (including but not limited to acquisitions, selection, purchasing, processing, storage, and de-selection)). From this project, I gained an understanding of the wide variety of formats that materials come in and the different ways in which they are used, as well as how to best store and protect them.

My family is from Webster County and I’ve done some research there before, so I appreciated being able to spend a little more time and learn more about WCGS from a professional perspective, as well as being able to give back to an organization that has helped me in the past. I also took the knowledge I gained from this project and used it to evaluate the emergency plan that we have at the library where I work, and I realized that we could benefit from having a few reusable flood barriers in our emergency response kit. Being prepared for emergencies is one of the best ways to make sure library materials are available for as long as possible.


## Professional Development

Library of Congress. (December 3, 2020). The preservation and presence of textiles in library and museum colletions: A panel discussion [Video]. YouTube. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tDGqQLqW7NE
Moderated by Charlotte Giles and Jonathan Loar with presentations made by Dan Paterson, Eiichi Ito, Kathy Woodrell, Maria Fusco, and Yasmeen Khan.

Overall, I thought the webinar had a lot of interesting content. The Library of Congress has extensive collections and the speakers clearly enjoyed sharing some of the textile-based treasures that they are responsible for. Although there were a few places I would have really liked better sound quality, this was recorded less than a year into the pandemic, and I'm sure everyone was still ﬁguring things out.

Three Things I Learned
- Library of Congress staff aren't necessarily trained in textile preservation the same way they're trianed in "book, paper, and photo conservation" (22:36), but there are plenty of examples of textiles in a wide variety of LOC collections. Knowing how to properly handle them is useful no matter what department a conservator works in.
- It isn't always obvious that textiles are a part of an item that we normally consider being made exclusively of paper or other materials (34:02). Conservators have to take care they don't damage one part of an item when trying tno repair or preserve another.
- Pest management is taken very seriously at George Washington University and The Textile Museum. They have an Integrated Pest Management program focused on prevention using monitored traps, building improvement, isolation and freezing/anoxic treatment of incoming materials, and high housekeeping standards (46:30).

Two Questions I Have
- What kind of textile expertise is preferred for LOC staff and what training on textiles does LOC provide to them?
- What kinds of insects can damage textiles/books? What are their life cycles, what attracts them, and how are infestations managed?

One Thing I Will Do Differently
- I intend to read the Four Corners of the World blog, especially the article about the Hmong cloth story maps, and examine the digitized version of the story maps themselves, if they're still available.

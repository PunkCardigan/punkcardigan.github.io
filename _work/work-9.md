This project was a collaboration with the Pimeria Alta Historical Society in Nogales, AZ, to create metadata based on the guidance provided by the project archivist, for a collection of videos they had digitized. The videos are historically significant to the area and could help researchers and community members learn more about Nogales and the people who lived there in the past.

[Pimeria Alta Metadata(PDF)](/files/pimeriaaltametadata.pdf)

This project provided me with hands-on experience for competence 9, Technological Knowledge and Skills.
- 9A. Identify appropriate technologies and uses that support access to and delivery of library services and resources. I learned about one method for creating metadata that can be used by libraries for providing access to digital materials. I also further developed my understanding of the Dublin Core standards for metadata.
- 9B. Understand and navigate ethical and cultural considerations and impacts on library practices and community members when applying technology to library services and resources.Nogales is a border town in Arizona, and much of the materials I worked with involved Hispanic students and community members. It is always best practice to engage with the community if possible and to be respectful of their beliefs and wishes. Although I was not aware that any of the materials I was responsible for had a connection to families still living in the area—who still hold the copyright on their videos—it was something that I looked for when people’s names were mentioned.

Robust digital metadata standards are still being developed. This project allowed me to learn about the
Dublin Core standards and compare them to standards I am already familiar with and new standards
discusses in class readings. The dynamic nature of information means that creating modular schema that
can adapt to rapid change in technical and social principles is imperative to the longevity of anything
adopted as a standard. From my experience with this project, Dublin Core seems to do this well.

It’s very easy to take for granted that metadata will be a part of digital files. Many digital devices (e.g.
cellphones) create quite a bit of metadata automatically, but physical items that have been digitized do
not have that. Librarians need to carefully consider their decisions when creating metadata for digitized
items, especially if the full context of those materials has been lost. The Pimeria Alta project was an
excellent way to learn not only how to create metadata, but to show how context of those materials (or
lack thereof) affects the process, and thus their accessibility.

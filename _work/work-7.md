This assignment was a practical application of skills used to determine the quality of various sources a librarian might use to find information, including citation databases and a book reviewer on Goodreads.

Analysis and reflection

For competence 7, Research and Evidence-Based Practice, I selected a small practical assignment that illustrates subcategories 7C (Understand principles and issues evolving with research, including an awareness of how professional and cultural values may influence each stage of the research lifecycle, the barriers related to access to research, and the tension between research and its application to professional practice) and 7D (Understand the importance of engaging in the research foundations and scholarly communications that will enable continued professional growth, knowledge, and sharing).

There are many different metrics for assessing the quality of research, but from my limited experience, none of them gave a complete picture of how a particular paper fit in to the wider publishing ecosystem. It's also appropriate to remember that in a publish-or-perish academic environment, competition can be fierce for limited space in high-IR journals like Nature, Cell, and Science. Vetting the publisher is important, but a low-IR journal is not an indication of the quality of the research being done. That's where a skilled librarian is helpful!

Additionally, not all queries need to be (or should be) answered with peer-reviewed research. I don't need a double-blind experiment to tell me which of the newest mystery books will be a hit with my patrons, but if I know which influencers and reviewers have quality information or are used by my patrons, I can potentially figure out what TikTok trend is going viral and order the books they'll want before they ask for them. Part of finding quality reviewers is being a quality reviewer, and while I'm terrible at getting my NetGalley reviews in before the publish date, I enjoy contributing to the community anyway.
